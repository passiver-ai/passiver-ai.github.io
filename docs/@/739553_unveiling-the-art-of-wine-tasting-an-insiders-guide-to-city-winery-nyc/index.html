The Basics of Wine Tasting: What You Need to Know

Before we can fully appreciate the sensory experiences offered at City Winery NYC, it's essential to understand the basics of wine tasting. Here are some key points to keep in mind:

  1. Sight: The first step in wine tasting is observing its appearance. Hold your glass up against a white background and take note of its color and clarity. Is it deep red for red wines or pale yellow for whites? Are there any sediments or bubbles present?

  2. Smell: Next, swirl your glass gently to release the aromas trapped inside. Stick your nose into the glass (don't be shy!) and inhale deeply. Take note of any fruitiness, floral notes, spices, or even earthy scents that you detect.

  3. Taste: Finally comes the moment we've all been waiting for – taking that first sip! Allow the wine to coat your palate evenly before making judgments about its taste profile. Pay attention to sweetness, acidity, tannins (for reds), body/texture/mouthfeel as well as flavors like fruits or oak.

Deciphering The Language Of Vineyards And Grape Varieties In NY State

New York State is not only known for its stunning landscapes and bustling cities but also for its thriving wine industry. With a wide range of grape varieties grown in different regions, the state has become a hub for vineyard practices that produce exceptional wines. But navigating through the world of grape varieties can be overwhelming. Fear not! This blog post will serve as your guide to understanding the language of vineyards and grape varieties in New York.

Navigating Through The Grape Varieties In NY State

  1. Riesling: Originating from the Rhine River Valley of Germany, Riesling is a cold-hardy white grape variety that thrives in northern vineyards of New York State. It is predominantly grown in Finger Lakes, Long Island, and Lake Erie AVAs (American Viticultural Areas). With 849 acres in Finger Lakes, 59 acres in Long Island, and 42 acres in Lake Erie dedicated to Riesling cultivation, this grape variety produces fresh and aromatic wines with complex flavors ranging from apple to honey.

  2. Chardonnay: Hailing from Burgundy region of France, Chardonnay is highly vigorous and cold-tolerant Vitis Vinifera grape variety cultivated extensively in Finger Lakes and Long Island AVAs. Spanning across 351 acres in Finger Lakes and 440 acres on Long Island, Chardonnay yields light to medium-bodied wines with rich flavors like apple, melon, peach blended with hints of butter or hazelnut when oak-aged.

  3. Gewürztraminer: Originally from Tyrol region of Italy but finding its home mainly within Finger Lakes AVA; Gewürztraminer is best suited for cooler climates due to its low cold-hardiness levels compared to other Vitis Vinifera grapes grown here primarily because it contains spice tropical fruit aroma with high alcohol levels.

  4. Catawba: A native North American variety, Catawba is a winter-hardy and vigorous grape grown in Finger Lakes and Lake Erie AVAs. Known for its spicy white or pink wines with crisp acidity, Catawba carries a characteristic Vitis Labrusca "foxy" aroma similar to grape jelly.

  5. Cayuga White: Developed at Cornell University, Cayuga White is an early-ripening French-American hybrid that thrives in Finger Lakes AVA. With 338 acres dedicated to its cultivation, this grape variety produces medium-bodied white table wines with balanced acidity and aromas of green apple and cotton candy.

  6. Traminette: Another French-American hybrid developed at the University of Illinois; Traminette shares flavor characteristics similar to Gewürztraminer but has distinct aromas of tropical fruit, floral notes, and spice.

  7. Sauvignon Blanc: Originating from the Bordeaux region of France, Sauvignon Blanc grows well in various soil types predominantly on Long Island where it covers 17 acres producing aromatic wines ranging from grassy to fruity flavors like melon, papaya blended with passionfruit undertones.

  8. Delaware: As one of the lesser-known varieties within NY State vineyards; Delaware possesses light pink to white wine color which signifies fruity yet spicy aroma lacking "foxy" flavor compared to other Vitis Labrusca grapes.

  9. Cabernet Franc: This moderately vigorous red grape variety can be found mainly in Finger Lakes and Hudson River Region AVAs spanning over 236 acres and 215 acres respectively producing light-medium bodied red wines carrying fruity or vegetal aromas such as strawberry or bell pepper notes.

10 Merlot & Pinot Noir are two cold-hardy yet delicate varieties thriving both on Finger Lakes & Long Island regions - while Merlot being characterized by roundness & soft tannins mixed black fruit, earthy and floral notes; Pinot Noir offers black cherry and spice aromas combined with earthy flavors.

A Peek Into The Vineyard Practices Of New York

Grape varieties may steal the spotlight when it comes to wine production, but vineyard practices play a crucial role in ensuring the quality of grapes. Here are some key vineyard practices employed in New York State:

  1. Viticulture: Viticulture refers to the cultivation and management of grapevines. In New York State, viticulturists focus on selecting grape varieties that are well-suited for their specific regions' climate conditions. This includes considering factors such as cold-hardiness, disease resistance, and ripening time.

  2. AVAs (American Viticultural Areas): AVAs are designated geographical regions recognized for their unique characteristics that influence grape-growing conditions. Finger Lakes, Long Island, Lake Erie, Hudson River Region, Niagara Escarpment are some prominent AVAs in NY State known for their diverse microclimates and soil types.

  3. Acreage: The acreage dedicated to each grape variety provides insights into its popularity among winemakers across different regions within New York State. For example Riesling is widely grown with 849 acres in Finger Lakes alone while only less than 10 acres being used for certain grape varieties like Delaware or Saperavi

4 Vinification: Vinification refers to the process of turning grapes into wine through fermentation and aging processes winemaking techniques vary depending on desired style from cool/cold stainless steel tanks preserving delicate flavors aromatic white wines full-bodied reds aged oak barrels adding complexity flavor profiles.

Unlock Secrets To Perfectly Balance Your Meal With Suitable Wines

Pairing food with wine isn't just about finding flavors that complement each other; it's also about creating balance on your palate. Here are some secrets to help you achieve perfect harmony between your meal and the wine at City Winery NYC.

1. Match Intensity Levels

When pairing food with wine at City Winery NYC or anywhere else for that matter, it's essential to consider the intensity levels of both elements. If you have a rich dish like steak or lamb chops, opt for a full-bodied red wine that can stand up to the flavors. On the other hand, delicate dishes like seafood or salads pair well with lighter white wines.

2. Consider Acidity

Acidity is a crucial factor when it comes to food and wine pairing. Acidic foods like citrus fruits or tomatoes tend to go well with high-acid wines as they balance each other out. For example, a crisp Sauvignon Blanc pairs beautifully with a tangy tomato-based pasta dish.

3. Think About Texture

The texture of your food should also be taken into account when choosing the right wine at City Winery NYC. Creamy and rich dishes are best paired with wines that have some weight and body, such as oaked Chardonnay or Merlot. Lighter dishes, on the other hand, benefit from wines that have more acidity and freshness.

4. Don't Forget about Sweetness

If you're indulging in dessert at City Winery NYC, it's important to choose a wine that complements its sweetness level without overpowering it. Rule of thumb: the wine should always be sweeter than the dessert itself! For instance, a luscious Sauternes pairs beautifully with crème brûlée or apple pie.

5. Experiment and Have Fun!

At the end of the day, food and wine pairing is all about personal preferences and experimenting with different combinations at City Winery NYC events will help you discover your own unique palate preferences. Don't be afraid to try something new – who knows? You might stumble upon an unexpected but delightful pairing that becomes your new favorite!

Getting Acquainted With World Class Sparkling Wines

Sparkling wines have long been associated with celebrations and special occasions, but they deserve much more than just being popped open during a toast. These effervescent delights come in various styles, each offering its own distinct characteristics that cater to different preferences. From crisp and refreshing Prosecco to rich and opulent Champagne, there's something for everyone.

  1. Prosecco: Let's start our journey with Italy's beloved bubbly - Prosecco. Made primarily from Glera grapes in the Veneto region, Prosecco is known for its lively bubbles, delicate fruit flavors, and vibrant acidity. It pairs wonderfully with light appetizers or can be enjoyed on its own as an aperitif.

  2. Cava: Spain has its own sparkling gem called Cava. Crafted using traditional methods like Champagne, Cava offers exceptional value without compromising on quality. It showcases notes of citrus fruits, green apple, and toasted bread crusts – making it an ideal choice when paired with seafood dishes or tapas.

  3. Champagne: Ahh...the king of sparkling wines! Champagne needs no introduction; it has been synonymous with luxury for centuries. Produced exclusively in the Champagne region of France using Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, and Pinot Meunier grapes, Champagne boasts unparalleled elegance and complexity. Its fine bubbles dance on the tongue, revealing layers of flavors such as citrus, brioche, and stone fruits. It's a perfect companion for celebrations or to elevate any moment.

  4. Crémant: France has more to offer beyond Champagne – enter Crémant! This sparkling wine is produced in various regions across France using different grape varieties like Chardonnay and Chenin Blanc. Crémant wines are known for their affordability while still maintaining high quality. They showcase lively acidity, delicate fruit aromas, and creamy mousse – ideal for pairing with seafood or light poultry dishes.

  5. Sparkling Rosé: Let's not forget about the delightful world of pink bubbly! Sparkling rosés come in a range of styles from dry to sweet and can be made using various red grape varieties like Pinot Noir or Grenache. These wines exhibit vibrant fruity notes like strawberries and raspberries balanced by refreshing acidity - a true delight when paired with salads or desserts.

Now that we've covered some of the most popular types of sparkling wines let's move on to exploring the uniqueness of dessert wines.

Understanding Uniqueness Of Dessert Wines

Dessert wines are often overlooked but hold immense potential in terms of flavor profiles and versatility. These luscious libations offer an indulgent experience that perfectly complements desserts or can even be enjoyed on their own after dinner.

  1. Sauternes: Hailing from Bordeaux, Sauternes is revered as one of the finest dessert wines in the world. Made from Semillon, Sauvignon Blanc, and Muscadelle grapes affected by noble rot (Botrytis cinerea), Sauternes displays an enchanting balance between sweetness and acidity. Honeyed apricot flavors mingle with hints of caramelized sugar - creating a symphony on your palate that pairs beautifully with foie gras or blue cheese.

  2. Port: Portugal's gift to the wine world, Port is a fortified wine that deserves a place on every wine lover's table. Produced in the Douro Valley, Port offers an array of styles ranging from dry and nutty Tawny Ports to rich and robust Vintage Ports. These wines boast intense flavors of dark fruits, chocolate, and spices - making them an excellent choice alongside decadent chocolate desserts or aged cheeses.

  3. Ice Wine: Canada has made its mark in the dessert wine realm with its renowned Ice Wines. Crafted from grapes left on the vine until frozen, these wines offer a concentrated burst of sweetness balanced by vibrant acidity. Ice Wines showcase flavors of tropical fruits like mangoes and pineapples – pairing divinely with fruit-based desserts or creamy cheesecakes.

  4. Late Harvest: Late Harvest wines are made from grapes left on the vine longer than usual, allowing them to develop higher sugar levels while maintaining natural acidity. This winemaking technique results in lusciously sweet wines with rich flavors of dried fruits, honey, and caramelized sugars – perfect for sipping after dinner or accompanying tangy fruit tarts.

  5. Moscato d'Asti: Italy brings us Moscato d'Asti – a light-bodied sparkling dessert wine known for its aromatic qualities and lower alcohol content compared to other dessert wines. With notes of orange blossoms, peaches, and apricots combined with gentle bubbles, this refreshing treat pairs beautifully with fresh fruit salads or fruity sorbets.
