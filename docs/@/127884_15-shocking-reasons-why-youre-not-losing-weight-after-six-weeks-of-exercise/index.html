Quashing Common Myths About Age, Metabolism & Stubborn Flab

Age is often blamed for the slowdown in metabolism and the stubborn flab that seems impossible to lose. Why 'Age-Related' Slowdown Is A Misconception That's Holding You Back

Have you ever wondered why it feels like losing weight becomes harder as you get older? Many people believe that their metabolism slows down with age, making it more difficult to shed those extra pounds. However, recent findings challenge this long-held belief.

Metabolism refers to all the chemical processes in our bodies that allow us to sustain life. It includes converting energy from food into energy for essential tasks like breathing and digesting food. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) measures the minimum amount of energy needed while fasting and at rest.

According to a study conducted by Fatima Cody Stanford and Chika Anekwe, there are four distinct phases of adjusted total and basal energy expenditure over our lifespan: neonatal phase (1 month to 1 year), childhood and adolescence (1 to 20 years), adulthood (20 to 60 years), and older adulthood (>60 years).

Neonatal Phase: Energy Expenditure Similar To Adults?

Intriguingly, neonates in their first month of life already have size-adjusted energy expenditure similar to adults! From birth until around one year old, energy expenditure rapidly increases, peaking at around 0.7 years old. Between nine and fifteen months, subjects have energy expenditures nearly 50% higher than adults. So much for the idea that metabolism slows down as we age!

Childhood and Adolescence: Growing Pains and Increased Expenditure

During childhood and adolescence, both total and basal expenditure continue to increase with age. This is due to ongoing growth processes such as building muscle mass. However, size-adjusted expenditures actually decline throughout this period, with no increase during puberty (ages 10 to 15). These findings suggest that the belief in a significant metabolic slowdown during adolescence may not hold water.

Adulthood: Stable Metabolism Regardless of Age

Contrary to popular belief, the study found that from ages 20 to 60 years old, total and basal expenditure along with fat-free mass remain stable regardless of sex. Adjusted Total Energy Expenditure (TEE) also remains stable during pregnancy. So even when you're knee-deep in adult responsibilities or expecting a little one, your metabolism doesn't necessarily slow down.

Older Adulthood: Declining TEE & BMR

It's only around age sixty that adjusted TEE and BMR start declining along with fat-free mass and fat mass. The decline is gradual but consistent at approximately 0.7% per year after reaching this milestone birthday. By the time someone reaches ninety years old or beyond, their adjusted total expenditure can be up to about 26% lower than middle-aged adults.

The study authors also explored how physical activity level and tissue-specific metabolism contribute differently across different ages:

  • In early life (neonatal phase), elevated tissue-specific metabolism might relate more closely to growth or development.
  • In later life (older adulthood), reduced energy expenditure could reflect organ-level metabolic decline.

So what does all this mean? It means that blaming age for your weight loss struggles might not be entirely fair! While there are some changes in metabolism as we get older, they may not be as significant or universal as we once thought. Other factors like physical activity level and organ-specific metabolic changes also play a role.

The Takeaway: Don't Let Age Define Your Fitness Journey

The idea that age is the primary cause of weight gain or slowed metabolism can be discouraging. It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking that there's nothing you can do about it. But remember, these are just myths!

The key takeaway from this study is that individual variations in energy expenditure should be considered alongside age-related changes. While our bodies may undergo some metabolic shifts throughout life, maintaining a healthy lifestyle with balanced nutrition, regular exercise, enough rest, and stress management remains crucial for overall well-being and weight management.
